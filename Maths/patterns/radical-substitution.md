# Radical subsitution problems
## The problem:

$$
    \sqrt{x^2+1}+x^2+1=90
$$

## My attempt
I first tried substituting:
$$
    k= x^2 +1
$$
which gave out $\sqrt{k} + k = 90$, after squaring on both sides. $k^2+k+2k\sqrt{k} = 90^2$

## What I realised
If both $ A \quad \text{and} \quad \sqrt{A} \quad $ appear substituting,
$$ k=\sqrt{A} $$
is often effective beacuse, we get: 
$$
    k + k^2 = 90   
$$ as $k=\sqrt{x^2+1}$ and $x^2+1 = k^2$ 

## solving

$$k^2 + k = 90 $$
$$k^2 +  10k - 9k - 90=0 $$
$$k(k+10) -9(k+10) = 0 $$
$$(k+10) (k-9) = 0 $$

Since roots cannot be negative 
$$k=9$$
as $k=\sqrt{x^2+1}$, 
$$9^2=x^2+1$$
$$80=x^2$$
$$x=\pm4\sqrt{5}$$


when $A
\quad \text{and} \quad
\sqrt{A}$ appear in the same expression, substituting the square root can simplify the structure significantly.
